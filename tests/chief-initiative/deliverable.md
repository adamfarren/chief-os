# Business Case: Managed Services Line of Business

**Initiative Lead:** Jordan Reyes, VP Customer Success | **Budget Envelope:** ~$800K/year

[SIMULATED — all data is fictional test data for Meridian Ledger]

---

## 1. The Thesis

Meridian Ledger sells financial operations infrastructure. Our customers buy the platform to automate their close — but a meaningful segment, particularly businesses under $500K ARR, lacks the internal capacity to implement it. They buy the vision and stall in onboarding. That stall creates churn, negative word-of-mouth, and a gap between what the platform can do and what customers actually experience.

A managed services line of business would change the relationship: instead of handing customers a platform and a knowledge base, we operate the platform for them. We handle configuration, workflow design, change management, and ongoing optimization — and we charge a recurring fee for doing so.

This creates three things the core software business cannot create on its own:

1. **A profitable retention instrument.** Customers in managed services don't churn — they're embedded. Every churn event we prevent pays back the implementation cost within 6 months.
2. **A proprietary implementation playbook.** Running implementations at scale across 20–30 customers builds an asset no competitor can copy: a proven, repeatable deployment methodology with benchmarked time-to-value. That playbook eventually becomes a product (certifications, partner program, self-serve onboarding tools).
3. **A proof-of-concept for the next product tier.** Managed services is the R&D environment for what eventually becomes an Enterprise tier with SLA-backed implementation guarantees, dedicated CSMs, and premium support. The LOB validates the economics and the workflow before we build the product layer on top.

Now is the right time. Meridian Ledger has 87 customers and a clear pattern: the sub-$100K ARR segment churns at 3× the rate of the mid-market. That's a solvable problem — and the solution funds itself.

---

## 2. Strategic Rationale

**The sub-$100K churn problem is the most preventable revenue leak in the business.** Meridian Ledger's churn data shows that customers under $100K ARR churn within 18 months at a rate 3× higher than the $100K–$500K segment. The dominant cause is not product dissatisfaction — exit surveys point to "couldn't get fully implemented" and "didn't have someone to call." These are not product failures. They are onboarding failures. Managed services addresses the root cause directly.

**Without this, the sales motion becomes self-defeating.** The current go-to-market strategy targets smaller businesses to build density in key segments. But if those businesses churn at high rates, every new logo is partially offsetting an existing loss. The net retention math doesn't work. Managed services converts the smallest-customer segment from a churn risk into a stable recurring revenue base — and gives sales a differentiated answer when prospects ask "what happens after we sign?"

**Jordan Reyes is the right person at the right time.** Jordan has spent 18 months building the CS function and knows where every implementation has stalled. She has the operational instincts to build the playbook and the credibility with customers to sell the value of the service. The alternative — hire an external leader — delays the launch by 6–9 months and introduces ramp time. Using Jordan now, with a backfill on her current IC CS responsibilities, is the faster and lower-risk path.

**This converts a cost center into a P&L.** Customer success is currently overhead. Moving Jordan into an initiative with its own revenue, its own margin targets, and its own breakeven timeline transforms the team's incentive structure. They stop optimizing for "customers are happy" and start optimizing for "customers are getting ROI" — which is the metric that drives renewal.

## 3. Operating Model

### 3a. Service Selection

The initial offering is a fixed-scope, 90-day implementation package priced at $2,000/month with a 12-month minimum commitment ($24,000 ACV). Scope: platform configuration, workflow design for 3 core use cases, staff training, and a 90-day optimization sprint post-go-live. After Month 3, the customer transitions to a lighter-touch "managed optimization" tier at $1,500/month.

**Why this scope first:** It covers the exact gap that drives churn — failed implementations — without attempting to solve every customer need. It's narrow enough to be repeatable and wide enough to demonstrate value.

**Sequencing:** Launch (months 1–6) → optimize the playbook against 10 customers → build self-serve tooling for the highest-volume implementation tasks (months 7–12) → introduce a premium tier with SLA-backed guarantees and dedicated CSM (Year 2).

**Constraints:** Requires 2 FTEs minimum to maintain quality. Cannot scale beyond 20 active managed customers per CSM without degrading service quality. This caps Year 1 at approximately 25–30 active customers without additional hiring.

### 3b. Capability/Technology Stack

| Function | How It's Done | Status |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| Implementation scoping | Jordan Reyes + playbook template | Build (Month 1) |
| Platform configuration | CS team using existing admin tools | Live |
| Workflow design & documentation | CS team + Notion templates | Build (Month 1) |
| Training delivery | Live sessions + recorded walkthroughs | Partially live — expand |
| QBRs and optimization reviews | Quarterly calls, manual prep | Manual — automate Year 2 |
| Customer health monitoring | Existing CS tooling (Gainsight) | Live |
| Billing and invoicing | Finance team via existing Stripe integration | Live |
| Renewal management | CS + AE collaboration | Live — formalize handoff |

### 3c. Key Hire

**Role:** Customer Success Manager — Implementations (CSM-I). Backfills Jordan's existing IC responsibilities and serves as the second delivery resource for managed services.

**Why critical:** Jordan cannot build the playbook and run live implementations simultaneously. Without a second person, she becomes the bottleneck within 60 days. The CSM-I is what converts Jordan's time from firefighting to building.

**Must be exceptional at:** Running structured implementations under time pressure. Converting messy customer workflows into clean configurations. Holding customers accountable to their commitments without damaging the relationship.

**Does not need to be:** Technical (the platform handles complexity). A strategist. A product thinker. This is an execution role.

**Profile:** 3–5 years in SaaS implementation or CS. Experience with workflow-heavy software (ERP, finance ops tools, or similar). Strong project management instincts. The CSM-I will be Jordan's operational partner — they need enough confidence to run implementations independently when Jordan is building the business.

**Compensation:** $90–$110K base + performance bonus tied to managed customer NPS and retention.

### 4b. Classification

**Internal division, not a separate entity.** Managed services operates as a line of business inside Meridian Ledger, Inc. Jordan leads it as a P&L owner with monthly revenue and margin targets, but there is no new legal entity, no new equity, no separate banking.

**Why this classification:** The LOB does not require regulatory separation, does not have liability exposure that needs to be ring-fenced, and does not benefit from spin-out optionality at this stage. Adding entity overhead would cost $15–30K in legal setup and introduce accounting complexity with no corresponding benefit.

**Disclosure:** Not proactively surfaced to investors as a new product line. If asked, it's a customer success investment with a revenue component — accurate and not misleading. Once the LOB reaches $500K ARR and has a proven playbook, it becomes a feature of the Series C narrative, not a footnote.

## 6. Questions to Stress Test

### 6a. Questions to Ask Yourself

1. **Key person risk:** If Jordan leaves or underperforms, this initiative has no leader and no playbook. The CSM-I hire mitigates delivery, but the strategic build stalls. What is the 90-day continuation plan if Jordan exits in Year 1?

2. **Narrative risk:** Positioning Meridian Ledger as a services company alongside a software company can confuse the product story for investors and prospects. The classification choice (R&D, not a product line) manages this — but it needs to be airtight before Series C diligence surfaces it.

3. **Focus tax:** Jordan owns the CS function for 87 customers. Moving her focus to building a new LOB means something in current CS coverage slips. What specifically gets deprioritized, and does it affect renewal rates or expansion revenue in the core business?

4. **Kill criteria:**
   - Month 3: If fewer than 8 active MSCs and NPS below 7, pause new sales and diagnose the playbook
   - Month 6: If monthly retention below 90% or CAC above $1,500, the unit economics don't support scaling — evaluate restructure or exit
   - Month 12: If MSCs below 25 or cumulative net below -$250K fully loaded, the business model is not working — exit costs ~$150K in severance and wind-down

### 6b. Questions to Ask Others

**Jordan Reyes (initiative lead):**
- What does she need to see in the financial model, the structure, and her comp to say yes?
- Does she want the CSM-I backfill hired before she transitions, or after?
- What is her 30-day plan — which 5 customers does she want for the pilot cohort?

**Sales team:**
- Will managed services create channel conflict (AEs get credit for MSC deals vs. CS owns them)?
- Can AEs reference managed services as a differentiator in deals with implementation-heavy prospects?

**Finance:**
- How does the managed services revenue get recognized? Ratably over the contract term (likely) or on delivery milestones?
- Does the LOB have its own cost center from Day 1, or does it roll up to CS overhead until it reaches a revenue threshold?

**Existing customers (pilot cohort):**
- Will the 5 pilot customers see this as a fee for something they currently get for free?
- What is the value proposition for customers who are already live — is managed services for struggling implementations only, or also for customers who want to go deeper?

## 7. Enterprise Structure

### 7a. Entity Options

| Structure | Pros | Cons | Recommendation |
|-----------|------|------|----------------|
| Internal division | Simple, no legal overhead, P&L tracked inside existing entity | No liability separation, no spin-out optionality | **Recommended for now** |
| Wholly-owned subsidiary | Clean P&L, limited liability, potential spin-out | $15–30K setup, ongoing accounting complexity | Revisit if LOB reaches $1M ARR |

### 7b. Ownership and Control

Managed services is operated by Meridian Ledger, Inc. directly. No separate ownership structure is required because: (a) there are no regulatory requirements for separation in software services, (b) the liability exposure is standard professional services risk covered by existing E&O insurance, and (c) the economics are not complex enough to warrant a separate entity at this stage.

### 7c. Key Agreements

No new inter-company agreements needed. The MSA between Meridian Ledger and each managed services customer governs the relationship. Existing customer contracts should be reviewed to confirm they permit Meridian Ledger to provide implementation services directly (vs. requiring third-party implementers). If any contracts prohibit this, amend before launch.

### 7d. Financial Consolidation

Managed services revenue and costs consolidate into Meridian Ledger's P&L directly — no special accounting treatment. Revenue recognized ratably over the contract period. Jordan's comp is reallocated from the CS cost center to the managed services cost center in the chart of accounts.

### 7e. Cap Table Impact

None. No new equity issued. No dilution to existing shareholders. Jordan does not receive a separate equity grant for this initiative — her existing option package reflects her total contribution to Meridian Ledger.

### 7f. Regulatory Flags

- **Professional services liability:** Managed services creates professional services obligations. Meridian Ledger's E&O insurance should be reviewed to confirm it covers implementation and configuration services, not just software. If not, add a professional services rider.
- **No industry-specific regulatory requirements** for software implementation services in the fintech space beyond standard SOC 2 / data protection (this is not regulated financial advice — no broker-dealer or fiduciary licensing issues apply).
- **Data access:** Managed services CSMs will have admin access to customer environments. Confirm that existing data processing agreements and customer contracts cover Meridian Ledger employees accessing customer financial data for implementation purposes.
